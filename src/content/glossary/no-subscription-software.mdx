## The Rise of the Subscription Model

Beginning around 2010–2015, most major software vendors migrated from one-time sales to subscription models. Adobe, Microsoft, Intuit, and dozens of productivity tool makers switched from perpetual licenses to monthly or annual plans.

For vendors, the economics are clear: a user paying $12/month generates more revenue over 5 years than one who paid $99 once. For users, the change means software is never truly owned.

## Why No-Subscription Matters for Personal Tools

For professional enterprise software, subscriptions often make sense — teams need coordinated access, IT support, and regular feature development. For personal productivity tools, the calculus is different:

- A personal budget tracker doesn't need team collaboration features
- A home document organizer doesn't need real-time cloud sync
- A health tracker doesn't need server-side AI processing

When personal tools are subscription-based, users pay for infrastructure they don't need and subsidize features built for enterprise customers.
